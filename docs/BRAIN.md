# The BRAIN: The Informal Brain Behind a Formal Mathlib

> Proposal for Jack (lead) and Fable (orchestrator). Produced 2026-07-03 by a multi-agent research
> workflow (4 data-verification agents + 6 web+repo research streams, deepened + adversarially
> critiqued) grounded in the live repo. Companion artifacts: `catalog/data/hierarchy.json` (the
> Level 1-3 backbone), `catalog/build_hierarchy.py`, the live `/map` + `/map-v2`.

## Vision

WikiLean's north star is to be the informal brain behind a formal Mathlib: a hierarchical, locality-scoped knowledge graph of all mathematics (and CS, and physics) whose every node is grounded in a formal implementation and legible to both humans and AIs. Mathlib and its sibling libraries (FLT, Physlib, Cslib, Carleson, SpherePacking, ...) are the formal skeleton — 388,105 decls across 39 libraries, 11.3M typed dependency edges (catalog/.cache/statement_formal.csv, formal_dependency.csv). The BRAIN is the connective tissue: it names those decls with Wikidata concepts, places each concept at its correct altitude (a field-of-study like Category Theory lives at the module level; a single theorem lives at a leaf), attaches informal statements (arXiv, textbooks, from TheoremGraph) alongside the Lean decls that formalize them, and lets humans and AIs edit and refactor that placement through GitHub login — exactly the way WikiLean's annotation moderation already works.

We do not build this from scratch. Every piece has a foothold in the repo: /map and /map-v2 already render circle-pack + force-directed views of 2,581 formal-first nodes and 84,445 weighted edges; hierarchy.json already carries a mechanically-derived Level 1-3 backbone (39 libraries, 2,711 subfields); source_registry.json already models 12+ external databases as layered provenance; the better-auth + revisions + propose-then-approve + findLostHuman stack already governs human/AI co-editing of annotations. The BRAIN is the act of joining these into one addressable, editable, AI-native structure and extending it past Mathlib to every library.

The framing that keeps this grounded and prevents slop: the formal graph is ground truth, the informal graph is a legible index over it, and the two are joined by verified edges — never by vibes. A concept earns its place in the BRAIN by resolving to a real decl (decl-existence oracle) or a judge-affirmed arXiv match, and every human/AI refactoring of that placement is versioned, diffable, and revertible. The BRAIN is Wikipedia's legibility with Mathlib's correctness, and it is designed so a 1000-agent build can populate it without a curator in the loop for every node — humans and the propose-then-approve queue gate only the placements the machine is unsure about.

**Feasibility.** Buildable NOW, but only as a hybrid, not as pure graph-theoretic clustering. Three findings converge on the same architecture: (1) Module-root is a clean, near-deterministic placement signal WHERE a formalization exists (94.3% single-module among the 1,202 formalized nodes) — this directly operationalizes the user's principle (a) "right altitude": a concept whose formalizations all live under one Mathlib.X.* root is a LEAF at that module's altitude; a concept with formalizations spanning multiple module roots (only 68 cases, a manageable human-reviewable list) is itself a MODULE/BRIDGE-altitude concept and should be promoted, exactly the Q217413 pattern the user described. (2) Global community detection (Louvain/Leiden/label-propagation) on the formal-dependency graph is NOT feasible as a primary placement mechanism: it was tested (both vanilla and with hub-excision) and it reliably collapses 80-99.7% of connected nodes into one blob, because Mathlib's proof-dependency graph is a near-transitive-closure through foundational lemmas (Set, Order, Real, Nat, Equality) — every non-trivial theorem eventually cites them, so edge density doesn't separate subject-matter, it just measures 'how core is this to the whole library.' This is actually useful signal (a good centrality/importance score, which manage/centrality.py already computes via PageRank) but the WRONG signal for topical clustering. (3) The already-built atlas_taxonomy.json + build_atlas.py deterministic cascade (module→MSC→neighbor-vote→unsorted) already achieves 68.9% coverage on a smaller prior graph and is legible to humans (curated continent names, frozen ordering per the file's own comment) and to AI agents (small enough to be a lookup table, not a live computation) — this is the RIGHT primary mechanism, and it should be re-run against concept_graph_v2.json's fuller node set to refresh coverage numbers (not done in this session; the 1,477-node atlas_data.json predates the 2,500-node v2 rebuild). Where community detection IS legitimately useful: as a LOCAL, bounded-radius operation for the "unsorted" 31% remainder and for the /map "explore locally" bubble expansion (principle b) — e.g. compute label-propagation or a simple k-core restricted to a node's 1-2 hop neighborhood at click-time, not globally upfront. This matches the existing /map Bubbles view's circle-pack-containment design rather than fighting it.

**Risks:** The 68 module-span concepts are a promising signal but a small sample — worth eyeballing all 68 by hand before trusting the 'promote to module/bridge altitude' rule; a concept could span 2 modules by grounding error (bad decl match) rather than genuine conceptual breadth. decl-existence sweep memory (decl_existence_sweep.py, ~5.5% stale rate found in a recent audit) suggests some fraction of formalizations[] entries could be wrong, which would corrupt this signal. · 48% module coverage / 40% isolated-node rate means any clustering or placement pipeline built today only directly serves about half the graph; the other half needs either more grounding work (growing formalizations[]) or a fundamentally different informal-only signal (Wikidata subclass-of chains, MSC codes) — don't oversell 'clustering is solved' when coverage is the binding constraint. · Local/bounded clustering (the recommended approach) has NOT been prototyped end-to-end in this session — only global label-propagation was tested and shown to fail. The bounded-radius variant is a reasonable extrapolation from the failure mode (dampen by radius instead of by hub-excision) but should be built and eyeballed on a handful of seed QIDs before wiring into /map, since 'looks reasonable in theory' and 'looks reasonable to a human clicking through bubbles' are different bars. · atlas_taxonomy.json is explicitly frozen/hand-curated per its own _comment field ('beloved maps freeze their coarse structure... continents and their ordering must not drift between builds') — any proposal to let data-driven clustering override or reshuffle the 9 continents would violate a design decision already made deliberately; new clustering should ADD substructure (subfield-level or below) or handle the unsorted remainder, not replace the continent scaffolding. · No graph libraries (networkx/igraph/leidenalg) are installed, and centrality.py's docstring suggests this is a deliberate 'pure Python, no dependencies' policy for the deterministic side of the pipeline — if a real Louvain/Leiden pass is ever wanted for the unsorted remainder, that's a new dependency decision Jack should make explicitly, not something to slip in via pip install during a coding session.

## The proposal

### Hierarchy: a strict containment tree (Levels 1-5) plus a cross-cutting ontology overlay

Adopt the Neo4j taxonomy-vs-ontology split as the schema's spine (verified precedent, replacing the earlier SKOS citation): a single property graph holding TWO edge families. TAXONOMY edges are strict single-parent containment — library -> module -> subfield -> concept -> statement — mechanically derived and mirroring OpenAlex's proven 4-level tree (Domain/Field/Subfield/Topic = 4/26/254/~4,500, entirely automated placement, no hand-curation). ONTOLOGY edges are many-to-many, typed, weighted, and explicitly non-transitive: depends-on (from the 11.3M formal_dependency edges), formalizes (QID->decl), relates (formal neighbours), cites (TheoremGraph arXiv links), classified-as (MSC). MSC and related-concept edges must NEVER become part of the containment tree.

Levels 1-3 already exist as hierarchy.json (catalog/build_hierarchy.py -> 39 libraries, 2,711 subfields), derived by splitting file_path on '/'. The backbone is real and cross-validated against the live mathlib4 checkout (31/31 Mathlib top modules match exactly). Two fixes make it production-grade: (a) MAX_DEPTH is a global 3, but Mathlib needs 5 — 55.3% of its 286,727 decls sit at file_path depth 4 and 29% at depth 5, so a global depth-3 truncates the natural nesting of a third of Mathlib; make MAX_DEPTH library-adaptive. (b) 5 of the 39 libraries (Aesop, Qq, Lake, ImportGraph, ProofWidgets) are Lean tooling, not math — tag each library library_kind: math|tooling|cs|physics so the Level-1 root selector (Math | CS | FLT | ...) excludes infrastructure by default.

Level 4 (concept/QID) and Level 5 (statement/decl) are the informal-brain layer. Level 4 nodes are the ~2,500 concepts already in concept_graph_v2.json, each carrying primary_decl + module. Level 5 (bare Lean decls) currently has NO dedicated surface — 'decl' is only a RESERVED slug string in index.ts, not a route. This is the one genuine structural gap.

**Concrete steps:**
- Make catalog/build_hierarchy.py MAX_DEPTH library-adaptive (Mathlib=5, small libs=2-3); re-emit hierarchy.json and add per-library library_kind (math|cs|physics|tooling) off a 39-entry allowlist.
- Wire hierarchy.json into site/build_map_data.py's json.load block (currently loads graph/atlas/reg/tg/descriptions only — verified by reading lines 73-80) so the concept panel breadcrumb shows the real library>module>subfield path.
- Define the two edge families explicitly in the graph artifact: edge.kind in {contains} (taxonomy, single-parent) vs {depends,formalizes,relates,cites,classified} (ontology, many-to-many, non-transitive).
- Add a minimal Level-5 surface: a /decl/:qualifiedName route (signature, docstring, defining module, QIDs citing it as primary_decl, formal_dependency neighbours) — even a lightweight page gives statements a first-class identity.

### Data foundation: the frozen TheoremGraph snapshot vs the live checkout, and multi-library grounding

The BRAIN's raw material is on disk: statement_formal.csv (388,105 decls / 39 libraries), formal_dependency.csv (11.3M typed edges), theorem_matching.csv (100,831 dual-judge arXiv<->Lean matches), and wikidata_universe.jsonl (11,681 math QIDs with cached P31 classes). Two data-integrity facts must drive the schema. First, the TheoremGraph CSV is a FROZEN mathlib4 snapshot, proven by drift: Mathlib/Std/Data/HashMap.lean appears in the CSV but no longer exists in the live checkout. So file_path-derived module links are safe as a STRUCTURE signal but must be freshness-checked against the live checkout before being used as clickable citation targets. Second, and most important for the vision: 100% of resolved QID->decl grounding (1,424 Mathlib + 37 Init in rebuild_grounding.json, confirmed by direct census) lives in Mathlib/Init. Zero QIDs resolve into FLT, Physlib, Cslib, SpherePacking, Carleson etc., despite those libraries holding ~35,000 decls. Multi-library coverage (vision principle c) is pure unstarted groundwork — structurally trivial (same file_path parsing) but currently empty.

The informal side has room to grow: math-graph ships informal_dependency.csv (18.3M arXiv theorem->theorem edges), statement_informal.csv, and slogan.csv (NL slogans per statement), none downloaded yet (~4-5GB each). Slogans in particular would give every Level-5 decl a one-line human-readable gloss — directly serving 'legible to humans and AIs.'

**Concrete steps:**
- Version-pin the TheoremGraph snapshot to its mathlib4 commit; add a nightly validation script diffing every module string against a live find *.lean per library, quantifying drift before using file_path as a link target.

# Adversarial critique: The BRAIN proposal

Ground-truthed against the repo (commands/files cited inline). Strong proposal overall — formal-graph-as-ground-truth is the right spine, and most numeric claims check out (388,105 decls confirmed via `csv.DictReader` census of `statement_formal.csv`; 39 libraries in `hierarchy.json`; 11,681 QIDs; `MAX_DEPTH=3` at `build_hierarchy.py:25`; `hierarchy.json` genuinely absent from `build_map_data.py`'s load block, lines 73-80). But there are factual errors, an unquantified core mechanism, and several missed techniques. Details below.

## 1. Factual errors that must be fixed before Fable acts

**(a) The `/decl` route already exists — the "one genuine structural gap" is wrong.** The proposal states (§Hierarchy) "Level 5 (bare Lean decls) currently has NO dedicated surface — 'decl' is only a RESERVED slug string in index.ts, not a route. This is the one genuine structural gap." **False.** `wiki/src/decl.ts` (7,885 bytes, dated Jul 2) registers `app.get("/decl/:name")` (line 149), imported at `index.ts:42`. It already: resolves name→current module via a nightly KV blob (survives Mathlib refactors); falls through to **non-Mathlib libraries** (CSLib, Physlib, Formal Conjectures) via `resolveInLibraries`; returns JSON with `cited_by` (every WikiLean statement citing the decl, via `citationsFor`, line 137); and has a live asset backend (`wiki/public/assets/decl-index/` = 852 shards). The P3 deliverable "/decl/:qualifiedName route (signature, docstring, module, citing QIDs, dependency neighbours)" is **~60% already shipped**. What's genuinely missing is narrow: **signature + docstring + formal_dependency neighbours** (the route currently redirects to Mathlib docs rather than rendering them, and doesn't surface dep edges). Fable should be told to *extend* `decl.ts`, not build a route — and this changes P3 from XL toward L.

**(b) The `RESERVED`/`findLostHuman`/`provenance` invariants are stated but not re-verified here.** The proposal leans hard on CLAUDE.md invariants. That's fine, but the concepts-table plan (§Editing) asserts `validation.ts`'s `diffFields` "already descends dotted paths… so it diffs altitude, cluster, related_concepts with zero changes." I did not see this verified against `validation.ts` in the research trail — and a nested-array field like `related_concepts` (a *list*, not a dotted object path) is exactly where dotted-path diffing typically breaks (list reordering → spurious diffs, or worse, a diff engine that treats `[a,b]`→`[b,a]` as no-op and silently drops a human edit — a `findLostHuman` 422 risk). **This is a load-bearing untested claim gating the entire P2 schema.** Flag it as "verify `diffFields` on array-valued fields before committing the concepts schema," not as settled.

## 2. The riskiest assumption: altitude recall is a showstopper the roadmap under-weights

The proposal is admirably honest that "P31 alone catches at most ~58% of true field-of-study cases." But it then makes altitude a **P1** deliverable and the per-agent workflow (P4) *depends* on `altitude_rule.py` proposing an altitude for every node. Do the arithmetic the proposal invites: ~58% recall on the highest-value, hardest decision, across ~11,681 QIDs, means **~4,900 concepts land at the wrong altitude** unless a human/model touches them. The proposal's escape hatch is "route low-confidence to propose-then-approve" — but with 58% recall the *high-confidence* bucket is small and the queue becomes the primary path, not the tail. **The 1000-agent-self-gating story quietly collapses into a mostly-human-adjudicated build.** Fable needs a number: what fraction of nodes clear the confidence gate unattended? If it's <50%, P4's "self-gating" framing is aspirational and should be relabeled "human-in-the-loop at scale."

Sharper fix: **make altitude a property of the ontology overlay, not a hard field.** Instead of committing one altitude per concept, store the multi-signal evidence (P31 class, match_kind, decl-collision flag, OpenAlex subfield-vs-topic spread) and let the *renderer* pick an altitude at semantic-zoom time. This defers the irreversible decision, makes it revisable without a re-run, and lets the 58%-recall rule be a *hint* rather than a gate.

## 3. Where it over-reaches vs the data

**(a) "Multi-library coverage is structurally trivial (same file_path parsing)."** The *hierarchy* is trivial. The *grounding* — QID→decl resolution into FLT/Physlib/Cslib — is not, and the proposal's own data proves it: 100% of resolved grounding is Mathlib/Init. The BRAIN's "formal graph is ground truth" premise assumes one coherent snapshot; mixing frozen-edges with live-modules breaks that coherence.
